# ODE Brand Voice and Tone Guide

## Purpose

This guide defines how ODE communicates across every touchpoint -- from website hero copy to enterprise proposals to tweet threads. Consistent voice builds trust. For an engineering platform, trust is everything.

---

## Voice Principles

ODE's voice is built on three pillars. These do not change across audiences or channels.

### 1. Technical but Accessible

We speak engineering fluently. We know what a Modelica connector is, what FMI co-simulation means, and why acausal modeling matters. But we never assume the reader has the same depth. We explain without condescending. We use precise terminology when it adds clarity, and plain language when it does not.

**Yes**: "ODE Component uses Modelica 4.0 for acausal, multi-domain modeling -- meaning you define the physics, and the solver figures out the math."  
**No**: "ODE Component leverages cutting-edge paradigmatic simulation methodologies for next-generation engineering workflows."  
**No**: "ODE Component does math stuff with your designs."

### 2. Confident but Not Arrogant

We believe ODE represents a genuine shift in how engineering software works. We say so clearly. But we respect the tools engineers already use and the expertise they already have. We never belittle competitors or dismiss legacy workflows.

**Yes**: "Legacy tools were not built for AI. ODE was."  
**No**: "Unlike those ancient, clunky tools you are stuck with..."  
**No**: "We might be a little better than existing options, maybe."

### 3. Innovative but Grounded

We are building the future, but we are not hand-waving about it. Every claim about AI capability is specific and demonstrable. Every feature reference is real. We do not promise what we cannot deliver. Innovation grounded in engineering rigor is our brand -- not hype.

**Yes**: "Claude AI integrated with ODE can generate Modelica models from natural-language descriptions and validate them against physical constraints."  
**No**: "Our revolutionary AI will transform everything about engineering forever."  
**No**: "We have some AI features in beta that might be useful."

## Tone by Channel

### Website (orthogonal.dev)

- **Tone**: Inspiring, clear, confident
- **Sentence length**: Short to medium. Punchy hero copy, clear explanations below.
- **Goal**: Communicate value in under 10 seconds, then reward deeper reading.
- **Headlines**: Declarative statements, not questions. Lead with what ODE does, not what it is.

### Blog / Long-Form Content

- **Tone**: Educational, thought-leading, generous with knowledge
- **Sentence length**: Variable. Technical depth is welcome.
- **Goal**: Establish ODE and Orthogonal as thought leaders in AI-native engineering.
- **Topics**: Engineering methodology, Modelica best practices, AI in simulation, open standards advocacy, customer workflows.

### Social Media (LinkedIn, X/Twitter)

- **Tone**: Concise, engaging, shareable
- **Sentence length**: Short. One idea per post.
- **Goal**: Drive awareness and traffic. Start conversations.
- **Format**: Bold claim or insight + brief support + CTA or link.
- **Hashtags**: Use sparingly. Prefer #Modelica #MBSE #AIengineering #FMI.

### Email (Newsletters, Onboarding, Sales)

- **Tone**: Personal, helpful, respectful of inbox
- **Sentence length**: Short. Scannable.
- **Goal**: Deliver value. Every email should teach, inform, or enable something specific.
- **Structure**: Clear subject line, one main message, one CTA.

### Sales Materials (Decks, Proposals, One-Pagers)

- **Tone**: Professional, value-driven, evidence-based
- **Sentence length**: Medium. Balance narrative with data.
- **Goal**: Build confidence in ODE as a platform investment, not just a tool purchase.
- **Structure**: Problem, solution, proof, next step.

### Documentation / Help Content

- **Tone**: Clear, precise, patient
- **Sentence length**: Short. Task-oriented.
- **Goal**: Help the user accomplish their goal with minimal friction.
- **Structure**: What, why (briefly), how (step by step).

## Writing Guidelines

### Sentence Structure

- Lead with the benefit or the action, not the feature name.
  - **Yes**: "Simulate multi-domain systems in your browser with ODE Component."
  - **No**: "ODE Component is a browser-based tool that lets you simulate multi-domain systems."
- Vary sentence length. Short sentences create emphasis. Longer sentences build context and nuance. Alternate deliberately.
- One idea per sentence. If a sentence has "and" connecting two independent clauses, consider splitting.

### Active Voice

- Use active voice by default. Passive voice is acceptable only when the actor is irrelevant or unknown.
  - **Yes**: "ODE generates Modelica models from natural-language prompts."
  - **No**: "Modelica models are generated by ODE from natural-language prompts."

### Jargon Policy

- **Use freely** (our audience knows these): Modelica, FMI, SysML, MBSE, co-simulation, acausal modeling, multi-domain, CAD, ODE (the product), differential equations, solver, parametric.
- **Use with brief explanation on first occurrence**: MCP Server, Modelica MCP, FMU (Functional Mock-up Unit), PLG (product-led growth), acausal vs. causal modeling (for broader audiences).
- **Avoid entirely**: Synergy, leverage (as verb), disrupt, paradigm shift, best-in-class, world-class, end-to-end (overused), holistic, ecosystem (unless literally referring to Modelica ecosystem), game-changer, revolutionary.

### Numbers and Data

- Use specific numbers over vague claims. "3x faster iteration cycles" beats "significantly faster."
- Cite sources or methodology for performance claims.
- Use numerals for all numbers in headlines and product copy. In long-form prose, spell out one through nine.

### Formatting Conventions

- **Product name**: ODE (always uppercase).
- **Tool names**: ODE Component, ODE Paper, ODE System, ODE Vision, ODE Geometry, ODE Interaction. After first mention in a section, the tool name alone is acceptable (e.g., "Component").
- **Company name**: Orthogonal.
- **Standards**: Modelica 4.0, FMI 2.0/3.0, SysML v1.6 (always include version on first reference).
- **AI references**: "Claude AI" on first reference, "Claude" subsequently. Never "the AI" or "our AI" generically.

## Do's and Don'ts

### Do

| Guideline | Example |
|---|---|
| Be specific about capabilities | "Claude AI generates valid Modelica 4.0 component models from plain-English descriptions." |
| Acknowledge engineering complexity | "Multi-domain simulation is hard. ODE makes the tooling part simple so you can focus on the engineering." |
| Respect the reader's expertise | "You already know Modelica. ODE gives you a faster, AI-augmented environment to work in." |
| Use concrete comparisons | "What used to take a week of manual model setup now takes an afternoon with AI-assisted generation." |
| Show, don't just tell | "Here is a thermal model Claude generated from a two-sentence prompt: [example]" |
| Credit open standards | "Built on Modelica 4.0 and FMI 2.0/3.0 -- your models are portable, not locked in." |

### Don't

| Guideline | Example to Avoid |
|---|---|
| Don't over-promise AI capabilities | "AI will design your entire system for you automatically." |
| Don't disparage competitors by name | "Unlike [Competitor X], which is outdated and overpriced..." |
| Don't use empty superlatives | "The most powerful engineering platform ever built." |
| Don't assume everyone is an expert | "Simply configure the DAE index reduction strategy..." (without context) |
| Don't use startup cliches | "We are disrupting the $50B engineering software market." |
| Don't be vague about what ODE does | "ODE helps engineers work better with AI." |
| Don't use fear-based messaging | "If you do not adopt AI now, you will be left behind." |

## Key Phrases and Vocabulary

### Preferred Terms

| Use This | Instead Of |
|---|---|
| AI-native | AI-powered, AI-enabled, AI-driven |
| Engineering platform | Engineering tool, software suite |
| Browser-based | Cloud-based, SaaS (in user-facing copy) |
| Open standards | Open source (unless referring to actual OSS) |
| Multi-domain modeling | Multi-physics (acceptable but less precise) |
| AI co-pilot | AI assistant, AI helper, AI bot |
| Design environment | Design ecosystem |
| Integrated | Seamless, frictionless (overused) |

### Approved Phrases (Use in Copy)

- "AI-native from the ground up"
- "Built on open standards"
- "Engineering reimagined with AI"
- "Your AI co-pilot for engineering design"
- "Six tools. One platform."
- "No installation. No lock-in."
- "From concept to simulation in minutes"
- "Physics-aware AI"

### Phrases to Avoid

- "Disruptive" / "Disrupting"
- "Game-changing" / "Game-changer"
- "Best-in-class"
- "One-stop shop"
- "Turnkey solution"
- "Digital twin" (unless in a specific, accurate technical context)
- "Democratize" (use in internal/investor materials; too abstract for product copy)
- "Unlock" (as in "unlock value" or "unlock potential")
